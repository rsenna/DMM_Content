Chapter 20 - Chords and Harmony

For some, the thought of chords and harmony can conjour up images of pop music but almost all electronic dance music records will employ harmony in one form or another. Indeed, harmony can provide the key element to many dance music genres from Uplifting Trance through to Deep House (seventh chords) and everything inbetween.

A Natural Progression

Chords moving from vi - ii - V - I

Composed in C Major, the harmony begins on a Submediant chord (vi) before moving onto the Supertonic (ii). The Submediant chord consists of the notes A-C-E whilst the Supertonic consists of D-F-A. Both these chords share a note (the A is the root of the Submediant and the fifth in the Supertonic), this provides a strong harmonic relationship between the two chords and therefore the movement between the two appears natural.

The Supertonic then moves into the fifth, the most powerful resounding note and chord in the key of the music. The movement from the dominant V chord to the I (the Tonic chord) is the most natural progression of all the chords due to the leading tone that is present in the dominant chord leaning towards to the Tonic.

Typical Chord Progression

Chords moving from I - IV - iii - V - I

A typical chord progression will employ a mix of strong and weak progressions. In the above example, the chords begin on the Tonic (I) and perform a strong movement into the Subdominant (IV). This is a particularly strong progression since both the I and vi share the note E and both are major chords

The progression then becomes weaker by moving from the Subdominant into the Mediant chord. Since the Mediant is a minor chord and shares no notes with the Subdominant it produces the weaker feel to the progression. This is, however, immediately rectified by moving into the Dominant chord (V). Since both Mediant and Dominant share the same note (B), the progression becomes strong again. The progression then employs the atypical V to I movement to complete the progression and cadence.

Symmetrical Progressions

Asymmetrical vs Symmetrical Rhythm

Symmetry between the chords rhythm through a progression is a vital yet often overlooked principle when composing a progression. In the above example the first chord structure exhibits an asymmetrical progression and results in an offsetting feel.

Directly following this is a secondary progression, consisting of the same chords but this time presented in a more symmetrical rhythm. This latter approach sounds more natural to the listener.
