Chapter 18 - Composing Drum Loops

Drum loops form the cornerstone of all electronic dance music but producing them with any degree of skill requires knowledge of a number of techniques. For this example, I'm using the atypical four to the floor rhythm, however the same techniques apply for any other style of loop (such as 2-step). In these cases, the only difference is the positioning of the kick and the snare, the processing techniques remain the same.

Basic "Four to the Floor" Rhythm

4/4 Drum Loop

The above example constitutes a typical 4/4 electronic dance loop. A kick occurs on every beat of the bar with a clap on the second and fourth. The loop is augmented with a 1/16th closed hi-hat pattern and an open hat on every 1/8th beat.

Reverb and Gate

Reverb and Gating

In this example, reverb has been applied lightly to the kick which results in a wider, deeper sounding kick with slightly less transient energy (I was aiming for slightly less transient - this can be controlled with the pre-delay on the reverb). Even though the kick employed a very short tail it was followed with a gate to ensure no reverb tail was present after the kick had ended. The clap was then treated to the reverse reverb technique.

Side-Chain Delay

Hi Hats SideChain Delay

For this example, The 16th closed Hi Hats are treated to delay on a bus, followed by compression. The original hats are fed into the side-chain of the compressor to prevent delay occurring during a hat strike. This prevents the hats from becoming clouded by delay but the same technique can also be used for reverb.

Snare & Filter Modulation

Cyclic Modulation of the Snare

In this example, I added a snare to layer with the clap (not necessary but it helped to thicken the timbre, some genres don't really call for this). However, regardless of the genre, the claps or snares should be treated to some cyclic modulation. Here I applied it to both the snare and the clap.

Cyclic Modulation on Hats

Filter modulation on the Hi-Hats

To add further interest to the loop, both closed and open hi-hats are treated to cyclic modulation via a filter. This slow movement creates a more interesting and diverse sounding loop.

Unequal Bar Division/Synopation

Asymmetrical Introduction

Nearly all genres of dance music will introduce further excitement into the drum loop by placing short transient sounds (commonly unusual sounding timbres) on asymmetrical positions within the bar. Here, I placed the effect on the 12th and 16th position of the bar. These were treated to a small amount of pitch modulation and delay.

Hemiola (Triplet)

Introducing Triplets

In the above example, I added a secondary hi-hat pattern but placed the transients on the triplet grid. This ensures they occur off the standard 16th grid and produces a skippy effect to the rhythm.

Swing

Applying Swing

To add further interest to a loop, its common practice in some genres (particularly forms of House music) to apply swing to the hi-hats. For this example, I applied swing to all of the hats and to the clap.

Complete Loop

The Complete Loop

The above example is the completed loop. I've kept this loop simple for the purposes of this exercise but layering further hi-hats in different patterns alongside layering instruments and employing syncopation and polyrhythm will all work to create more complex loops. In order to hear the loop in perspective, I've also added a bass and lead.

Parallel Compression

Introducing Parallel Compression

For the final example, I placed all of the drum elements on a group track and then "sent" the group track to a buss featuring a compressor. By adjusting the send amount, more or less parallel compression can be applied.
