Chapter 26 - Formal Structure

Beyond sound design, processing, effects and composition lies the formal structure or arrangement of the music. This can be viewed as the wrapping or method of delivering your ideas to the listening audience. However, since electronic dance music often relies on a single idea, its formal structure is unlike many other styles of music and relies on continual development of a theme rather than the atypical verse and chorus structure.

Percussive Call And Response

Call and Response In Percussion

Call and Response can occur in all instruments and in the more percussive orientated genres of electronic dance music (Techno, Tech House, Tech Step, Minimal etc) it is common to employ this in the percussive timbres. In the above example, the snare/clap has its own call and response system to add interest to the loop. This is slowly panned to the left during the course of loop to help you hear the effect.

Filters

Using Filters to Create Builds

Filters are perhaps the most fundamental tool in a producers arsenal when it comes to the production of an arrangement and building or releasing tension. In the above example, a low-pass filter opening produces the increasing tension to the mix.

Different Intros

Different Intros

In the above example, a reverse crash is used first to introduce the hi-hats. This is followed by a high pass filter employed on the sub bass that results in the higher frequencies of the bass entering the mix first. After this, the same style of high pass filter is employed on the open hi-hats, closely followed by a reverse reverb on the secondary bass.

Please note that in this example, I have kept all the introductions close together in order to reduce the examples time. In a real-world example, each of these introductions would likely occur 8 or 16 bars apart.

Bass Line Syncopation

Syncopation Through Velocity

In the above example, the bass line's frequency cut-off is mapped to respond to velocity (the harder the note is struck, the more the filter opens). Here, the first 8 bars maintain the same velocity but in the next 8 bars the second note in each bar is treated to a higher velocity.

The 8 bars following this have the third note of the bar treated to velocity and then for the final 8 bars, the fourth note in each receives velocity. Note how the frequency change creates a moving syncopation within the bass, introducing a new rhythmical feel every 8 bars.

Bass Line Pitch Development

Development of Pitch

In the above example, a reverse crash is used to introduce a change and this is followed by a pitch development in the bass line.

Syncopated Canonic

Canonic Movement

Listen carefully to the above example. The Canonic (the low frequency drone that is introduced) dictates the upcoming pitch motion of the bass whilst the bass itself remains at the same pitch. Later in the example, both the canonic and the bass converge, both moving in pitch. This is a popular technique in Swedish House but is slowly being adopted into other genres.

Automated Effects

Building Effects

Effects can also be used with building tension. In the above example, both reverb decay time and delay time were increased via automation. Listening to the effect, the reverb begins to swamp the lead timbre, producing an almost pad style feel to the lead. This technique is commonly used in progressive house.

NOTE: Typically this style of effect is underlined by an additional building effect such as a noise riser, drum or snare roll or reverse cymbal. I left these out so you could concentrate on the effect but I would suggest you trying this technique with further underlying elements.

Employing a Denial
